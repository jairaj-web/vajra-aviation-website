DGCA AME Training: Eligibility, Syllabus & Career Guide (2026)

November 5, 2025 Wg Cdr Srinivasan (Retd) Aircraft Maintenance

India's aviation sector is on one of the most ambitious expansion trajectories in the world — with over 600 new commercial aircraft on order and a fleet expected to nearly treble by 2035. Every single one of those aircraft must be certified airworthy before each flight. That certification is issued by a licensed Aircraft Maintenance Engineer (AME). If you are considering a technically rich, permanently in-demand career in aviation, DGCA AME training is one of the most direct routes to a lifelong profession.

This guide covers everything you need to know about DGCA AME training in India: what the licence actually means, the five licence categories, exact eligibility requirements, the full 10-module exam syllabus, exam format, career scope, salary benchmarks and why Bangalore is an excellent base for this career.

What is a DGCA AME?

An Aircraft Maintenance Engineer (AME) is a licensed aviation professional authorised by the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA), India, to certify that an aircraft — or a specific component — is airworthy and fit for flight after inspection, repair or scheduled maintenance.

In practical terms, the AME is the last line of technical defence between a maintained aircraft and the flying public. No commercial aircraft in India can legally depart without a licensed AME's certification signature on the maintenance release document. This statutory requirement makes the AME role one of the most safety-critical positions in civil aviation.

DGCA vs EASA Licensing

DGCA (Directorate General of Civil Aviation) is India's civil aviation regulator. A DGCA AME licence is legally valid for maintenance operations on Indian-registered aircraft. EASA (European Union Aviation Safety Agency) issues Part 66 licences recognised across Europe and by many international regulators. The two systems share a similar module structure (both derived from JAR-66), but examinations, approvals and the scope of privileges differ. Many experienced Indian AMEs hold both licences to access the global MRO market — Vajra Aviation offers training for both.

Key Fact

India's MRO market is projected to reach USD 4.3 billion by 2030, and domestic airlines currently send a significant share of heavy maintenance work abroad due to a shortage of licensed AMEs inside India. That skills gap is a direct career opportunity for candidates entering DGCA AME training today.

DGCA issues AME licences under CAR-66, aligned with international standards. There are five primary categories. Understanding each will help you choose the right training pathway.

Category Full Name Scope
B1.1 Turbine-powered aircraft — Mechanical Airframe, engines, mechanical systems and electrics on turbojet/turboprop aircraft. Most in-demand category for commercial airline MRO.
B1.3 Piston-engine aircraft — Mechanical Airframe, engines and mechanical systems on piston-powered aircraft. Common for GA and flying club maintenance.
B2 Avionics Avionics, electrical systems, instruments and communications equipment on all aircraft types. Rapid growth area due to advanced avionics suites.
C Base Maintenance Issue of Certificate of Release to Service for heavy base maintenance checks (C-checks, D-checks) on commercial aircraft. Requires prior B1/B2 experience.
B3 Light Sport Aircraft Piston-engine non-pressurised aircraft below 2,000 kg MTOM. Applicable to LSA, ultralight and small training fleets.

For candidates targeting commercial airline careers, B1.1 (turbine-mechanical) and B2 (avionics) are the primary tracks. IndiGo, Air India, SpiceJet and Akasa Air all recruit extensively from these two categories.

Eligibility for DGCA AME Training

The eligibility requirements are clear and accessible to students from both science and engineering backgrounds.

Minimum Academic Qualification

  • 10+2 with Physics, Mathematics and English from a recognised board (CBSE, ICSE or State Board)
  • Minimum 50% aggregate marks in Physics and Mathematics (requirements may vary by institution)
  • No age bar — candidates of any age may apply for the DGCA AME licence examination

Two Pathways to DGCA AME Licence

  • Pathway 1 (Apprenticeship): Complete a minimum 3-year approved apprenticeship at a DGCA-approved Maintenance, Repair and Overhaul (MRO) facility or airline, then appear for the DGCA AME examinations. Ground classes at an institute like Vajra Aviation prepare candidates for the exams alongside their apprenticeship.
  • Pathway 2 (Diploma / Degree + Exams): Hold a 3-year engineering diploma or 4-year B.Tech/B.E. in a relevant engineering discipline, complete the mandatory practical experience period, then clear the DGCA AME exams. This is the preferred route for B.Tech graduates entering AME training.
Can B.Tech / B.E. graduates apply?

Yes. Engineering graduates in Mechanical, Electrical, Electronics or Aeronautical Engineering are eligible. Certain modules may be exempted based on academic transcripts, reducing the number of exams required. Speak to the Vajra Aviation admissions team for a personalised module exemption assessment.

DGCA AME Exam Syllabus — All 10 Modules

The DGCA AME examination syllabus follows a modular structure. Candidates appear for individual modules as per their chosen licence category. Below is a summary of all 10 foundation modules relevant to B1 and B2 category candidates.

Module 1 — Mathematics

Arithmetic, algebra, geometry, trigonometry, calculus and statistics as applied to aircraft maintenance engineering problems. Strong foundation here underpins all subsequent technical modules.

Module 2 — Physics

Matter and energy, mechanics (statics, dynamics, fluid dynamics), thermodynamics, optics, wave motion and sound. Directly applicable to understanding aircraft structural loads, engine thermodynamics and hydraulic systems.

Module 3 — Electrical Fundamentals

Electron theory, static electricity, DC circuits, resistors, capacitors, inductors, transformers, AC theory, motor and generator principles, and aircraft batteries. Core knowledge for both B1 and B2 candidates.

Module 4 — Electronic Fundamentals

Semiconductors, diodes, transistors, integrated circuits, printed circuit boards, and basic analogue/digital electronic theory as applied to aircraft systems.

Module 5 — Digital Techniques and Electronic Instrument Systems

Number systems, data conversion, digital circuits, microprocessors, data buses (ARINC 429, ARINC 629, MIL-STD-1553), avionics software concepts and fibre optics. Particularly relevant to B2 avionics candidates.

Module 6 — Materials and Hardware

Aircraft materials (aluminium alloys, composites, titanium, steel), fasteners, pipes and hoses, springs, bearings, transmission components and the corrosion and treatment of aircraft structures.

Module 7 — Maintenance Practices

Safety precautions, workshop practices, hand tools, precision measuring instruments, avionics test equipment, electrical wiring and bonding, riveting, pipelines and hose assemblies, maintenance documentation and DGCA maintenance record requirements.

Module 8 — Basic Aerodynamics

Atmosphere, airflow over aerofoils, lift and drag, flight controls, high-lift devices, stability and control, theory of flight for both subsonic and transonic flight regimes. Foundation module for all AME categories.

Module 9 — Human Factors

Human performance and limitations, social psychology, factors affecting performance, physical environment, tasks and communication — all within the context of aircraft maintenance. A critical safety module that addresses maintenance error causation models.

Module 10 — Aviation Legislation

ICAO Annexes, Indian Aircraft Rules 1937, DGCA Civil Aviation Requirements (CARs), airworthiness directives, continued airworthiness framework, approved maintenance organisations (AMOs), maintenance programme requirements and AME licence regulations.

DGCA AME Exam Pattern

Understanding the exam format is essential for effective preparation.

Aspect Details
Format Computer-Based Test (CBT) conducted at DGCA-designated examination centres
Total Modules 72 modules total across B1 and B2 categories (number varies by sub-category)
Pass Mark 75% in each module — one of the highest passing thresholds in any professional licensing exam
Retake Period Candidates who fail must wait 90 days before reattempting the same module
Module Validity A passed module is valid for 5 years — candidates must complete all required modules within this window
Question Types Multiple choice questions (MCQ); no negative marking in most modules
Why 75% Pass Mark Matters

The 75% threshold is deliberately high because an AME's certification error can directly threaten flight safety. This is not a general aptitude test — it demands genuine technical mastery of each subject. Structured ground coaching with experienced instructors, module-wise mock tests and rigorous DGCA question-bank practice are essential preparation tools. Ad-hoc self-study often leads to repeated module failures and significant delays to licensing.

Career Scope: Where Do DGCA AMEs Work?

A DGCA AME licence opens doors across a wide spectrum of aviation employers in India and internationally.

Commercial Airline MROs (Line and Base Maintenance)

  • IndiGo MRO — India's largest airline operates one of the most active line maintenance networks in Asia, with regular AME recruitment
  • Air India Engineering Services (AIESL) — the national carrier's engineering subsidiary handles heavy base maintenance, with workshops in Mumbai, Delhi, Kolkata and Nagpur
  • SpiceJet Technical Services — expanding maintenance capability at key Indian airports
  • Akasa Air — India's newest full-service carrier building its own line maintenance team
  • Air India / AIX Connect (formerly Air Asia India, now part of Air India group) — growing maintenance ecosystem including wide-body and narrow-body fleets

Defence and Government Aviation

DGCA-licensed AMEs are eligible for positions with organisations such as MHCAA (Military Helicopters Civil Aviation Approvals), the Airports Authority of India (AAI) for navigational aids maintenance, and several paramilitary aviation units.

Independent MROs and Third-Party Maintenance

India has a growing network of independent DGCA-approved MROs including Lufthansa Technik India, GMR Aero Technic, Air Works India, and others — all seeking licensed certifying staff.

International MROs (DGCA + EASA Dual Licence)

AMEs who hold both a DGCA licence and an EASA Part 66 licence (available through Vajra Aviation's Aviotrace Swiss partnership) can work at MROs in the UAE, UK, Singapore, European Union and other EASA-recognised jurisdictions. International MRO salaries are typically 2–3x Indian domestic packages.

DGCA AME vs EASA Part 66: Which Should You Choose?

This is one of the most common questions from students entering AME training. The short answer: if your goal is a career in India, start with DGCA. If you want global mobility, add EASA Part 66 alongside it.

The two licences share a common intellectual foundation — both are derived from JAR-66 — but differ in examination bodies, approvals, and the countries where each licence is legally valid. A candidate who holds both licences is among the most employable AMEs in the world market.

Frequently Asked Questions — DGCA AME Training

What is the minimum qualification for DGCA AME?

The minimum educational qualification for appearing in DGCA AME examinations is 10+2 (Higher Secondary) with Physics, Mathematics and English from a recognised board. Candidates must also complete the required practical experience period — either through a 3-year approved apprenticeship at a DGCA-approved MRO or airline, or through an eligible engineering diploma/degree plus practical training.

How many modules are there in the DGCA AME exam?

The DGCA AME examination follows a modular structure with a total of 72 modules across B1 and B2 categories, though the exact number a candidate must clear depends on the specific licence sub-category (B1.1, B1.3, B2, etc.) and any academic exemptions granted. The 10 core foundation modules (Modules 1–10) are common to most categories and form the basis of AME ground training.

What is the pass mark for DGCA AME exams?

The pass mark for DGCA AME CBT examinations is 75% in each individual module. This is a deliberately high threshold that reflects the safety-critical nature of an AME's certification responsibilities. Candidates who fail a module must wait 90 days before reattempting it. Rigorous preparation with structured mock tests is essential to consistently clearing modules at this standard.

Can I do DGCA AME after B.Tech?

Yes, absolutely. B.Tech or B.E. graduates in Mechanical, Electrical, Electronics, Aeronautical or related engineering disciplines are eligible for DGCA AME licensing. In fact, engineering graduates may be entitled to module exemptions based on their academic transcripts, potentially reducing the number of DGCA modules they need to clear. Many B.Tech holders choose DGCA AME as a direct route into airline engineering roles. Contact Vajra Aviation's admissions team to assess your specific exemption eligibility.

What is the salary of a DGCA AME in India?

DGCA AME salaries in India range from ₹3–5 LPA at entry level to ₹20 LPA+ for Chief AME or Quality Assurance Manager roles. Mid-level certifying staff with 3–6 years of experience and a commercial aircraft type rating typically earn ₹6–10 LPA. AMEs who also hold an EASA Part 66 licence and work at international MROs in the Gulf or Europe earn 2–3x the equivalent Indian package, often with tax-free status and accommodation benefits.
